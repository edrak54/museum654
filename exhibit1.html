Modern Art in Motion

by Emily Drachman

Futurism and Dada are both movements that are part of early modernism, specifically around the years of World War One. Futurism took place largely in Italy just before the start of the war while Dada began just after the war. Both groups were reacting to their circumstances in very different cultures despite such close temporal proximity. The Futurists, in an attempt to move past the classical history that was so revered in Italy, were all in on new technology and industry. The Futurists sought to glorify war in contrast to the Dada artists, working after the onset of WWI who were reacting to very real horrors that had previously been unimaginable. Dada is a reaction to a world which did not make much sense and thus often includes nonsensical elements in their work. Both groups push against norms and seek to disrupt accepted systems. Each brings a distinct and important perspective to understanding the time period during which they were working.

Futurism

The Futurist Manifesto

The Futurist manifesto was a founding document for Futurist art. It was published by the poet F. T. Marinetti in 1909. Futurism was focused on the future, new technology, and swift departure from all things past. Being an Italian movement, they felt that Italy was particularly focused on the classical past. The manifesto calls for a destruction of the past including museums, libraries, and academies. Writing just before World War One, they also call for the glorification of war and militarism. Their focus was on the future and technology and a destruction of all else.

Notable Artists

  • Filippo Tommaso Marinetti, 1876-1944
  • Gino Severini, 1883-1966
  • Carlo Carrà, 1881-1966

Dada

During the First World War, Dada was an art movement reacting to the horrors of that time. Hugo Ball was a writer and the founder of Dada. The art of the movement is united by anti-war sentiments and often used satire or nonsensical elements as critique of the war and a society which caused it.

Notable Artists

  • Kurt Schwitters, 1887-1948
  • Jean Arp, 1886-1966
  • Theo Van Doesburg, 1883-1931

Unique Forms of Continuity in Space

Umberto Boccioni, 1913, cast 1950

Umberto Boccioni’s sculpture, Unique Forms of Continuity in Space is one of the most emblematic pieces of Futurist sculpture. The sculpture depicts a man moving incredibly dynamically. The static sculpture has a forward moving energy to it. Not only is the man sculpted, but there is a suggestion of the environment around him in the air which he is displacing as he speeds along, particularly in the swirling shapes around his legs.

Cut with the Kitchen Knife Dada Through the Last Weimar Beer-Belly Cultural Epoch of Germany

Hannah Höch, 1919

Hannah Höch’s Cut with the Kitchen Knife Dada Through the Last Weimar Beer-Belly Cultural Epoch in Germany is a photomontage piece commenting on the Dada movement and the contemporary politics of Germany at the time. Photomontage and collage art was widely used amongst Dada artists. It allowed the appropriation of popular imagery and newspaper clippings and also suggested the fragmentary nature of culture as the Dada artists saw it. For this piece particularly, Höch uses the images of prominent figures, both fellow Dada artists as well as political figures. The composition is a bit chaotic and it can be difficult to parse through to recognize individual elements, but the piece can be read in quadrants. The top right shows the “anti-dada”, including various politicians at the time. The lower right is the Dada quadrant, including many prominent Dada Artists who Höch knew such as Raoul Hausmann, George Grosz, and Niddy Impekoven as well as various communist leaders. The left side shows a broader Dada movement. The top left includes Albert Einstein saying “Dada is not an art trend” implying the movement is larger than a passing trend in art and that it is political and is here to stay. The bottom left depicts crowds of people around the Communist Party leader Karl Lienbknecht, who is saying “join Dada!” Höch also includes herself in the composition, at the bottom right on the corner of a map which shows countries where women had the right to vote. Höch also points to the role of women in politics and art with her title, Cut with the Kitchen Knife, which implies an element of the domestic labor that falls on women.

Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash

Giacomo Balla, 1912

Giacomo Balla, another notable Futurist painter focuses on a study of motion in his Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash. The repetition and blur of leash and legs suggest the speed at which these figures are moving. The painting has a frenetic energy which suggests the dynamism which the Futurists thought contemporary everyday life should have. The painting is certainly influenced by early motion capture attempts including that by Etienne-Jules Marey, who was able to capture motion in a similar style photographically by including multiple exposures on one photographic plate. The effect of the repetition shows the various moments of a motion happening synchronously which creates the sense of movement in a static image.

Fountain

Marcel Duchamp, 1917 (photograph by Alfred Stieglitz)

Marcel Duchamp’s Fountain is often met with skepticism both today and when he first created the piece. This piece is one of his most notable “readymade” works, a kind of artwork where an artist selects a commercially available object and names it as art. Duchamp pioneered “readymades” and in doing so, posed questions about what art really is. The story of the fountain specifically is that Duchamp bought the porcelain urinal at a plumbing supply store and signed it “R. Mutt”. He submitted Fountain to an unjuried art exhibition which caused much debate among the Society of Independent Artists in New York. Ultimately the work was rejected from the show but was photographed and featured in The Blind Man, a magazine published by Duchamp and his friends. The article featured a defense of Fountain, stating that the selection and naming of the work placed it in a new context whether the artist physically made the object or not.
